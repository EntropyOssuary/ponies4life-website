3-Week Programme

Griffin & Dana
Introduction to Jumping

Coach: Kirstin  ·  Location: New York (indoors)

Horse notes: Griffin may have a slightly shorter stride. Working indoors also compresses distances slightly — adjust spacing as needed and monitor rhythm throughout. All distances listed in metres and feet.
Week 1 — Introduction to jumping
Day 1
Building patience & rhythm
Foundation session
1
Trot the vertical
Place a placing pole on the approach at 2.5m / 8'2" in front of the vertical. Trot in quietly, letting the placing pole set up the take-off spot. Keep the jump small — focus is patience and a correct, balanced approach.
Say out loud "one, two" every time you sit in the saddle during the trot approach. This keeps both you and Griffin focused on a consistent beat — if you lose the rhythm in your voice, you've lost it in your riding.
This exercise anchors every session. It teaches Griffin to sit on his hindquarters and wait rather than rushing or flattening.
Watch: Trotting the vertical
Between exercises — let Griffin walk and stretch on a long rein before moving on.
2
Canter poles on a circle
Set two canter poles evenly spaced on a large circle. Ride a balanced canter, counting the strides out loud and trying to hear the next stride coming. Griffin is your dance partner — you need to listen to each other to dance in step.
Encourages straightness, rhythm, and rider awareness of stride length on a curve.
Watch: Improve your rounds with the circle jump exercise
Day 2
Adding a landing rail & second jump
Introduce distance awareness
1
Trot the vertical (warm-up)
Repeat the placing pole trot exercise from Day 1. 2.5m / 8'2" approach pole. Establish the same quiet, patient rhythm — say "one, two" out loud to maintain the beat — before progressing.
Between exercises — let Griffin walk and stretch on a long rein before moving on.
2
Vertical → landing rail → second jump
From the trot vertical, add a rail on the ground on landing at 3m / 9'10", followed by a second jump at 3m / 9'10" from the rail. The landing rail is there to regulate the stride — it stops Griffin zooming off or flattening after the first fence and keeps him thinking forward in a controlled, measured way.
Keep both jumps small initially. The goal is rhythm and connection between fences, not height.
Day 3
Introducing the bounce grid
Engagement & collection
1
Trot the vertical (warm-up)
Always start here — builds patience and hindquarter engagement before grid work. Say "one, two" on the approach to set the rhythm.
Between exercises — let Griffin walk and stretch on a long rein before moving on.
2
Grid: vertical → bounce → rail on ground
Build progressively: trot vertical, then add a bounce, then add a rail on the ground at 3m / 9'10" after the bounce. Ride this combination approximately 8 times. Gradually raise the second jump (after the bounce) but keep the first jump smaller to maintain a soft, easy entry into the grid.
Indoors and with Griffin's shorter stride in mind — don't rush the height increases. Quality of footfall and rhythm is the priority.
Watch: Improve your horse's jumping style with a bounce grid
Between exercises — let Griffin walk and stretch on a long rein before moving on.
3
Canter poles on a straight line
Finish with canter poles on a straight line. Focus on keeping Griffin listening to a steady, consistent beat — count the strides out loud and try to feel the rhythm beneath you. Think of it as keeping the dance going, just on a straight line now.
A calm, rhythmical finish rewards the effort of the grid work and reinforces the connection built through the session.
Day 4
Triple bounce — sustained effort
Strength & engagement
1
Trot the vertical (warm-up)
Same as every session — patient trot approach, placing pole at 2.5m / 8'2". Say "one, two" on the approach.
Between exercises — let Griffin walk and stretch on a long rein before moving on.
2
Triple bounce grid
Evolve Day 3's grid by replacing the landing rail with a third bounce. Three bounces in succession creates sustained physical effort — do not increase height. The three-element combination demands more from Griffin physically and mentally than height alone.
Three bounces = sustained engagement. Bigger is not better here — the effort is already significant. Keep everything small and correct.
Watch: Improve your horse's jumping style with a bounce grid Watch: How bounces and oxers improve your horse's style
Between exercises — let Griffin walk and stretch on a long rein before moving on.
3
Small jumps on a circle — set on the centreline
Replace the canter poles on a circle with small jumps. Set these on the centreline rather than the outside track — this forces you to ride the corners actively and build the last few straight strides into each fence correctly.
Riding the corners well is a skill in itself. The centreline placement means you can't rely on the track to guide you — you must steer.
Watch: Improve your rounds with the circle jump exercise
Day 5
Grid into short course — figure-8
Putting it all together
1
Trot the vertical (warm-up)
As always — patient, quiet trot with placing pole at 2.5m / 8'2". Say "one, two" to establish the rhythm before every approach.
Between exercises — let Griffin walk and stretch on a long rein before moving on.
2
Grid into short course — figure-8 layout
Build from the grid into a short course using the figure-8 arena layout:
Left loop — Trot poles / cavaletti (drill) into an angled ground pole (bump)
Right straight line — Jump → 2 strides → 3ft jump → rail on ground 8'2" / 2.5m spacing (combination line)
Bottom loop — Single jump
This session ties together everything from the week — patience off the approach, distance awareness, rhythm through combinations, and confident forward riding.
